# MD13 — Privacy Concerns with AI Surveillance

**Date:** 04.11.2026 | **Instructor:** Professor Srikanth Bhakthan

## Problem Statement

Examine the real-world privacy and fairness harms caused by AI-powered facial recognition surveillance, using documented cases to argue for legislative, technical, and industry-level reform.

## Approach

Argumentative essay structured around the PEEL paragraph method, grounded in documented wrongful arrest cases and supported by peer-reviewed sources and policy reports.

**Core cases examined:**
- **Randal Reid (2022):** Spent six days in jail due to a facial recognition false match for a crime committed in a state he had never visited
- **Angela Lipps (2026):** Lost six months of freedom, her home, her car, and her dog after U.S. Marshals arrested her at gunpoint based solely on an AI match — bank records that cleared her were available within a week

**Argument structure:** Both cases share the same failure — treating a software result as a verdict rather than a lead. The essay argues this will keep happening without three parallel reforms: federal legislation (image retention limits, operator requirements, off-limits use cases), better technical standards (representative training data, pre-deployment bias audits), and industry accountability frameworks (Microsoft's Responsible AI principles cited as a replicable model).

## Results

The essay argues that AI facial recognition's bias, lack of federal oversight, and absence of accountability are not separate issues — they share a common root: a technology that moved faster than anyone's ability to govern it. The solution requires all three reform tracks working simultaneously, because fixing only one leaves the other two wide open.

## Key Findings

The most striking finding: both Lipps and Reid were cleared not by the systems that failed them, but by basic investigative work that should have happened before any arrest. The absence of a required verification step — not technical failure — was the proximate cause of harm in both cases.
